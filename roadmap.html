Interactive Prototype

Drag-to-Prioritize Roadmap

Static slides and spreadsheets go stale the moment the meeting ends. This prototype explores a living roadmap where priority is communicated by dragging — making the Now vs. Next conversation visible and instant.

Problem

▼

Product teams spend time maintaining roadmap slides that are outdated before they're shared. Async readers can't tell if a card moved since last week, or whether "Next" means next sprint or next quarter. A drag-to-reorder board makes priority a first-class interaction — the current state is always visible, and changing it takes one gesture instead of editing a deck.

How It Worked

▼

Three lanes — Now, Next, Later — answer the single most common roadmap question: is this in flight, committed for the next cycle, or backlog? Cards show a title, one-line description, and priority level. Drag to reorder within a lane, or drop into a different lane to change status; column counts update instantly. Reset restores the original state.

Designed for async consumption first: a stakeholder can share a link and the reader gets the current picture without scheduling a sync.

Tradeoffs

▼
  • Three lanes, not more: Adding On Hold, Blocked, or Done columns increases maintenance cost without improving the core priority conversation. Now/Next/Later is the minimum viable signal.
  • No due dates: Dates create false confidence and rot fast. Priority relative to other cards is more honest and easier to maintain.
  • No assignees on cards: Team tags communicate ownership without turning the board into a task manager. Assignee-level tracking belongs in Jira.
  • Drag-only edit: Adding inline content editing would shift focus away from the prioritization interaction the prototype is exploring.
  • Session-only state: Persistence requires auth and a backend — out of scope. The interaction model is what's being validated here.

Product Ownership

▼

Structure

Chose Now/Next/Later over sprint-based columns — maps to business priority, not delivery cadence, so it's readable by non-technical stakeholders.

Card data model

Title + one-liner + priority only. Deliberately excluded due dates, story points, and assignees to keep cards scannable at a glance.

Interaction spec

Drag inserts at position — not just appended to the end — so within-lane ordering carries meaning. Column counts update live.

Reset behaviour

Reset restores original card positions and order — intentional for demos where you need to walk through the interaction repeatedly without refreshing.

Outcome

▼

The prototype makes visible what a slide can't: the act of dragging is itself the prioritization conversation. "Should this move to Now?" is easier to answer when you're holding the card and can see what's already there. The board replaces a 30-minute status meeting with a 10-second async read.
