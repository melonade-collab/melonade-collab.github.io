Re-imagining

Web Accessibility Remediation

A 6-month effort to bring a wireless carrier's website to WCAG 2.1 AA compliance — coordinating fixes across independent product teams who each owned their own corner of the site.

Problem

▼

The carrier's website had accumulated years of accessibility debt across independently owned sections — screen reader users, keyboard navigators, and low-vision users hit real barriers on every part of the site. No single team owned it, so no team fixed it; it lost out to feature work in every prioritization conversation, and surfacing another team's violations required careful framing to avoid triggering defensiveness.

Constraints

▼
  • No central web team — individual product teams owned and deployed their sections independently, on their own schedules
  • Any fix could be reverted by another team's next deployment with no visibility into what changed
  • No shared design system enforcing accessibility standards across teams
  • Teams were on independent sprint cycles, making coordinated cross-section changes take months
  • Remediation had to integrate into existing team roadmaps — it couldn't be treated as a separate workstream that blocked feature delivery
  • Some teams were protective of design decisions they'd already shipped, requiring careful relationship management alongside the technical conversation

Product Ownership

▼

Audit & Scoping

Conducted a full WCAG 2.1 AA audit across all primary user flows. Categorized violations by type and severity, then mapped each issue to the owning team — making remediation actionable without duplicating effort across teams.

Stakeholder Alignment

Worked team-by-team to frame accessibility as quality work, not extra scope. Navigated the political sensitivity of surfacing violations in other teams' areas by centering the conversation on user impact and shared accountability rather than blame.

Training & Tooling

Designed and delivered accessibility workshops for product and design teams. Introduced Stark (Figma) and axe DevTools (browser) so teams could evaluate issues themselves — shifting the model from central remediation to distributed, ongoing prevention.

Governance

Worked with the design system team to embed accessibility into shared components so future fixes were inherited by default. Helped establish accessibility acceptance criteria as part of the definition of done across participating teams.

What Was Fixed

▼

Text contrast was the most pervasive issue — dozens of foreground/background combinations failed the 4.5:1 AA ratio, most caused by grey text on white or low-opacity text on dark backgrounds applied for aesthetic effect. Heading structure problems were also widespread: pages with skipped levels, missing H1s, or heading tags used purely for visual styling rather than document hierarchy.

Interactive elements had two recurring failures: icon-only buttons with no ARIA label, making them invisible to screen reader users; and focus indicators removed with outline: none to match brand aesthetics, which eliminated keyboard navigation visibility entirely. Alt text coverage was inconsistent — content images carried empty or auto-generated filename alt text, while decorative images were missing alt="" altogether. Form inputs throughout the site used placeholder text as a substitute for labels, which disappear when typing starts, are invisible to screen readers, and break when autofill is used.

Tradeoffs

▼

Distributed ownership without enforcement

Keeping remediation within each team's roadmap avoided a separate workstream, but meant no team held overall risk — a team could slip on compliance without anyone catching it until the next scheduled audit.

Design system embedding

Fixing shared components made future products inherit compliance by default, but existing products still needed manual remediation on their own timelines, with no mechanism to force it.

AA, not AAA

Targeting WCAG 2.1 AA was a deliberate call — it covers the broadest set of users within what's practically achievable — but some users still encounter barriers that only AAA requirements address, and that gap was explicitly accepted.

Training over automated guardrails

Shifting to distributed prevention through training is a culture change, not a technical constraint. Training fades without reinforcement, and no automated CI/CD check existed to catch accessibility regressions at merge time.

Outcome

▼

Brought the site to WCAG 2.1 AA compliance across all primary user flows over a 6-month remediation cycle. Subsequent quarterly audits found zero critical violations. Product and design teams began self-reporting accessibility checks as part of sprint reviews. The training program was absorbed into onboarding for new design and product hires — making prevention, not just remediation, part of the culture.
