Re-imagining

Shipping Assistant

Customers had no self-serve way to get a shipping rate or delivery time estimate without calling in and waiting for a live agent.

Problem

▼

Carriers handle high volumes of inbound calls from customers who only need a simple answer: how much will this cost, or how long will it take? There was no self-serve path. Customers had to phone in, navigate a menu tree, and wait for a live agent — for information that could be delivered instantly.

Constraints

▼
  • No account creation or login — had to work for any customer without requiring sign-in
  • Had to feel conversational and natural, not like filling out a rate calculator form
  • Needed to handle rate lookups, delivery time questions, or both in one flow
  • Required a clear path to live agent handoff for customers with more complex needs

Product Ownership

▼

Call flow analysis

Mapped the inbound call flow to identify the minimum information agents actually needed to answer a rate or delivery time question. Found it was almost always just destination and parcel weight class.

Conversation design

Designed the conversational flow to collect those two inputs naturally. Validated through testing that the format felt more like asking a person than filling out a rate calculator.

Agent handoff design

Defined the live agent handoff escape at the end of every conversational path — directly addressing the primary stakeholder concern about complex customer needs falling through the cracks.

Prototype & validation

Built the prototype end-to-end with simulated rate tables so testing could be done interactively rather than from static screens.

How it worked

▼
  • Mapped the inbound call flow to identify what information agents actually needed to answer a rate or delivery time question — almost always just destination and parcel weight class
  • Designed a conversational flow that collected those two data points naturally, without presenting them as a form
  • Added a live agent handoff escape at the end of every path so customers with more complex needs always had a clear way forward
  • Built the prototype end-to-end with simulated rate tables so the experience could be demoed interactively rather than shown as static screens

Tradeoffs

▼

Conversational scope

The flow handles common queries well but struggles with edge cases — unusual package types, third-party shipper situations, international addresses with special requirements — that a structured form would handle more robustly.

No session history

Without login, users who return for the same information start from scratch every time. Repeat queries were common in research but were explicitly out of scope given the no-account constraint.

Agent handoff cost

The handoff button at the end of every path provides a reliable safety valve, but routes users to a live agent — and its cost — even for queries that fell just outside the bot's scope and could have been handled differently.

Simulated rate tables

The prototype uses static rate data; real pricing engine integration would require handling account-specific discounts, fuel surcharges, and dimensional weight — a meaningful engineering lift not addressed here.

Outcome

▼

The prototype validated the self-serve concept: every internal tester completed a rate or delivery time lookup in under 90 seconds with no instruction. The conversational format consistently felt more natural than a form — testers described it as "like asking a person." The agent handoff button at the end of every flow resolved the biggest stakeholder concern about dropping complex cases. Business case modelling suggested 15–20% of inbound rate and time queries could be deflected with this flow.
