Re-imagining

Enterprise Mobile Self-Service

A B2B mobile app for corporate-liable employees to manage their company-provided phone plan — without knowing their account number, because it was never theirs to know.

Problem

▼

Enterprise employees on corporate plans had no self-serve app — checking data usage or changing a voicemail PIN meant calling B2B support or navigating an IVR, both of which drove significant inbound volume. The blocker was authentication: corporate employees are enrolled under the company's master account with no personal account number, making standard registration flows a dead end.

Constraints

▼
  • No account number — employees were sub-accounts under a corporate master; that number lived with IT or the company's account admin
  • Identity had to be derived from the SIM itself, pulling the subscriber number the carrier already had on file
  • SIM-based verification is device-specific — the app only works on the phone with the enrolled SIM, which was actually an intentional trust signal
  • Corporate accounts have a different data model than consumer accounts — plan features, billing, and contacts are all at the company level
  • Voicemail access had to route through the carrier's voicemail platform, not a consumer-facing system
  • The experience needed to feel professional and trustworthy for a business audience

Product Ownership

▼

Authentication Model

Defined the SIM-as-identity approach: on first launch the app reads the device's MSISDN and ICCID, verifies against the carrier's subscriber database, and surfaces the account without a login form. A friction-free registration that actually increases trust because the device itself is the credential.

Feature Scoping

Identified the three tasks that drove the most inbound calls to B2B support — usage checks, voicemail PIN changes, and plan details — and scoped the MVP around those rather than trying to port the full consumer feature set to an enterprise context where it didn't belong.

Voicemail UX

Designed a visual voicemail inbox that felt closer to email than to a phone IVR: caller ID, message preview via transcription, inline playback, and a clear path to change PIN without navigating a dial-tone menu.

Enterprise Context

Surfaced the corporate account relationship (company name, IT contact) so employees understood what plan they were on and who to escalate to for things outside the app's scope — closing the loop without overwhelming IT.

How It Worked

▼

On first launch, the app silently read the SIM identifiers from the device — no input required from the employee. A quick API call matched the SIM to the subscriber record in the carrier's system. If a match was found, the employee landed directly on their dashboard: name, number, plan, and current usage cycle.

The voicemail inbox displayed messages with caller information and an AI-generated transcription so employees could skim messages without listening. Tapping a message expanded an inline player with skip controls and a callback button. Changing the voicemail PIN was a three-field flow: current PIN, new PIN, confirm. All of this replaced calls to B2B support or a carrier IVR.

The Plan tab surfaced the features included in their corporate plan alongside device details pulled from the SIM — IMEI, SIM number — which were frequently requested for IT troubleshooting but previously required the employee to dig into phone settings.

Outcome

▼

Measurably reduced inbound call volume to B2B support for plan-related inquiries. The SIM-as-identity model became the baseline assumption for the enterprise product line — no login flow was ever designed because it was never needed. Voicemail PIN management — previously a call to IT or a carrier IVR — became a self-serve three-step flow inside the app.
