Re-imagining

Scan Event Ordering

Packages would show "Delivered" — then "Out for Delivery" minutes later. A root cause investigation and fix to ensure shipment scans always display in the correct sequence, regardless of when they arrive.

Problem

▼

Customers tracking shipments were seeing events displayed out of order on the public tracking page — most visibly, packages appearing as "Delivered" before "Out for Delivery" had shown up. Some tracking pages even showed the package leaving a facility before it had arrived. Customers were calling in confused and frustrated, and support ticket volume for tracking queries had risen sharply.

Constraints

▼
  • Scan devices in the field (handheld scanners, vehicle-mounted units) batch and transmit events on intermittent connectivity — arrival time is unreliable
  • Each scan device captures its own event timestamp at the moment of scan, but clocks across devices weren't perfectly synchronized
  • Some scan events were legitimately close together in time, making pure timestamp sorting insufficient
  • The fix needed to be backward-compatible — historical tracking data also had to display correctly
  • No changes to scan hardware or field workflows were in scope

Product Ownership

▼

Root cause analysis

Identified the pattern in support tickets: out-of-order events clustered around specific scan types (facility arrivals and delivery confirmations). Traced it to the system sorting on ingestion timestamp rather than event timestamp. Importantly, the underlying scan data was accurate — all events were legitimate — making this a display ordering issue, not a data integrity one.

Requirements

Defined the ordering logic requirements: primary sort on event timestamp, secondary sort on stage rank to handle timestamp ties or device clock drift, with suppression rules for duplicate stage events.

Engineering partnership

Worked with the backend team to design and validate the new sort query. Contributed test scenarios with deliberately out-of-order scan data to verify the fix handled real-world edge cases.

Validation

Confirmed the fix against historical shipments that had previously displayed incorrectly. Tracked support ticket volume post-release to measure improvement in tracking-related contacts.

How It Worked

▼

Every scan device records two timestamps: the event time (when the scan physically happened) and the received time (when the data reached the system). Devices in warehouses and delivery vehicles often batch transmit — a scanner with spotty connectivity might send 10 scans at once hours after they occurred.

Before the fix, the tracking page queried scans ordered by received time — meaning a scan that arrived late appeared at the bottom of the list regardless of when it actually happened. After the fix, the query sorts by event time first, with stage rank as a tiebreaker for cases where device clocks are slightly out of sync. A deduplication rule also suppresses a repeat of the same stage if a higher stage has already been received.

Outcome

▼

Tracking-related support contacts dropped measurably in the weeks following the fix. The change also applied retroactively to historical shipments, resolving confusing tracking histories without any re-processing. The fix has been in place for all new shipments since, and no further out-of-order display issues have been reported from this root cause.

Tradeoffs

▼

Event timestamp reliance

Sorting by event timestamp is more accurate than received timestamp, but still depends on device clocks being reasonably set — a scan device with a significantly wrong clock would still produce incorrect ordering.

Stage rank as tiebreaker

The stage rank tiebreaker handles clock drift well against the current scan taxonomy, but any future changes to the stage model would require reviewing and updating the rank ordering to stay correct.

Retroactive fix

Applying the new sort to historical shipments resolved confusing tracking histories, but users who had already seen and acted on incorrect ordering wouldn't benefit from the correction after the fact.

Duplicate stage suppression

Suppressing repeat stage events is correct in most cases, but a legitimate re-entry into a stage — such as a package returned to a facility after a failed delivery — requires handling separately to avoid hiding real events.
